About

The long view on education

For more than a decade I have moved between the classroom, the international organisation and the archive. I began as a primary teacher in Manchester, trained in comparative education and international development at Edinburgh, and spent seven years at the UNESCO Institute for Lifelong Learning designing policy dialogues and capacity-building across Africa and Asia.

That practice now sits alongside doctoral research in international history. The same question runs through both: what is education promised to do, who makes that promise, and what happens to it in institutions and in people's lives. My advisory work brings a historian's perspective to present-day reform; my research brings a practitioner's attention to how promises are actually delivered.

Research · PhD in International History and Politics

The Promise of Education

Kenya in the World, 1945–1980

Geneva Graduate Institute, Department of International History and Politics · Supervisor: Professor Aidan Russell

"The aim must be to produce at all levels of African society morally sound, economically valuable citizens…" African Education in Kenya (the Beecher Report), 1949

In Kenya, education was repeatedly held out as the surest route to a better future — and the content of that promise was set as much abroad as at home.

This thesis is a transnational history of how Kenya's ties to colonial, bilateral and multilateral actors repeatedly reworked the promise of education for development between 1945 and 1980, and what that meant for institutions and for lives. It brings together two literatures that seldom meet — histories of development and international organisations, which watch from the centre and rarely reach Kenya, and histories of Kenyan education, which keep the wider world at the margin — to follow a single promise from its international making to its local effects.

The central question

How did Kenya's transnational relationships repeatedly redefine the promise of education for development between 1945 and 1980, and with what consequences?

The "promise of education" framework

The research is organised by a "promise of education" framework: an interdisciplinary, historicised reading of education as a dispositif — a heterogeneous ensemble of discourses, institutions, regulations and practices with a strategic function. Drawing on development scholarship that engages Foucault directly (Escobar, Ferguson, Rose, Li, Brigg), it traces the shifting relations of knowledge, power and subjectivity through which a development apparatus entrenches itself — and follows four successive versions of the promise: the colonial, the redemptive, the Africanising and the productivist.

Chapter outline

Six substantive chapters, each reconstructing one reworking of the promise as it was articulated and built, enforced and contested, or worked through in particular institutions and communities.

Chapter 1

Colonial education and the developmental subject

The educational settlement codified by the Beecher Report of 1949 and the developmental subject it imagined.

Chapter 2

Mau Mau, re-education and international scrutiny

The civilising promise enforced under Emergency conditions — and monitored transnationally.

Chapter 3

African agendas and national reforms

The Africanising promise as independence reshaped national education.

Chapter 4

Harambee and its transnational sponsorship

Self-help schooling and the outside actors who sponsored it.

Chapter 5

Productivity, manpower and education for the economy

The productivist promise: schooling recast as economic input.

Chapter 6

The university between national service and intellectual freedom

Higher education caught between developmental demand and academic autonomy.
