Alex Howells

I am a historian and education specialist whose work bridges the history of education, decolonisation and development with today's debates on international organisations and the global governance of education.

I am drawn to how education changes over time: what earlier reformers set out to do, what became of it and what that can tell us about the choices education faces now and the directions it might take next. Before my doctoral research at the Geneva Graduate Institute, I worked at the UNESCO Institute for Lifelong Learning in Hamburg, coordinating policy dialogues, workshops and research.

About

Looking at education over time

For more than a decade I have moved between the classroom, the international organisation and the archive. I began as a primary teacher in Manchester, trained in comparative education and international development in Edinburgh, and worked at the UNESCO Institute for Lifelong Learning in Hamburg, coordinating policy dialogues, workshops and research across Africa and Asia.

That experience now sits alongside doctoral research in international history and politics at the Geneva Graduate Institute. The same question runs through all of it: what is education promised to do, who makes that promise and what becomes of it in institutions and in people's lives. I am interested in what earlier efforts attempted, what came of them and what they can tell us about the choices facing education today.

Research · PhD in International History and Politics

The Promise of Education

Kenya in the World, 1945–1980

Geneva Graduate Institute, Department of International History and Politics.

"The aim must be to produce at all levels of African society morally sound, economically valuable citizens…" African Education in Kenya (the Beecher Report), 1949

This study investigates the promise of education as a transnationally influenced, continuously redefined series of possibilities for Kenyans between 1945 and 1980.

It considers how different futures were proffered through education from colonialism to independence and a new era of global governance, with a particular focus on how relationships with the wider world reshaped the promise and its effects on institutions and lives in Nairobi and neighbouring Kiambu County. The study is organised around successive reworkings of what education would achieve for the people of Kenya.

The central question

How did Kenya's transnational relationships repeatedly redefine the promise of education for development between 1945 and 1980, and with what consequences?

Research framework

The research is organised around a framework devised for this project. It treats the promise of education as a dispositif, in Michel Foucault's sense: a heterogeneous ensemble of ideas, institutions, policies, plans, material forms and practices that cohered around a shared conviction that education would deliver development, and with it a better future. Each reworking is explored synchronically, reconstructing the configuration of actors, institutions and relationships that produced it, while the promise is also followed diachronically, tracing how unrealised ambitions, retained practices, raised expectations and institutional legacies accumulated over time. The study is sensitive to dreams and ambition and looks back unironically.

Reworkings of the promise of education

Across the period, the promise was repeatedly remade as Kenya's relationships with the wider world changed and as new understandings emerged of what education should achieve. The study follows these successive reworkings.

Colonial education and the developmental subject

The educational settlement codified by the Beecher Report of 1949 and the developmental subject it imagined.

Mau Mau, re-education and international scrutiny

The civilising promise enforced under Emergency conditions, and monitored transnationally.

African agendas and national reforms

The Africanising promise as independence reshaped national education.

Harambee and its transnational sponsorship

Self-help schooling and the outside actors who sponsored it.

Productivity, manpower and education for the economy

The productivist promise: schooling recast as economic input.

The university between national service and intellectual freedom

Higher education caught between developmental demand and academic autonomy.

A series hosted by Alex Howells

The Reform Cycle

Conversations on education's past and future.

Over the past sixty years, education has carried some enormous promises: universal schooling, education for development, Education for All. The Reform Cycle returns to the people who made and lived those promises, from the architects and officials to the teachers and students, to ask what they believed was possible, what stood in the way and what remains of it now. Each conversation looks back in order to see the present more clearly.
