The Reform Cycle · Episode 1

A Degree by Watching the Telly

Sir John Daniel on how the promise of open learning conquered the world and lost its audience — and what its record says about the promises now being made for AI.

In conversation with Alex Howells. Recorded over Zoom between Vancouver and Lyon. Quotes are lightly edited for readability and appear with Sir John’s approval.

When Harold Wilson proposed a University of the Air in the mid-1960s, the most generous reaction available was laughter. “Even those in his own party thought it was a pretty silly idea,” Sir John Daniel recalls. “Getting a degree by watching the telly — these kind of things. People were pretty skeptical.”

▶ Listen — “a degree by watching the telly”

Within a decade, the joke had inverted. Britain had two television channels, and the BBC broadcast Open University programmes in prime time, which meant that an entire country was incidentally auditing a degree. “People would look at this and say, good grief, I can do that maths they’re teaching — so maybe I can get a degree too.” Daniel was told at one point that one in four people in the UK knew someone who was, had been, or was related to an Open University student. And then the detail he plainly relishes, as an Oxford graduate: “Within a short time, OU came to mean Open University in the general parlance rather than Oxford University.”

This is the story of one of the largest promises ever made in British education: that higher learning could be opened to everyone the system had passed over, at scale, at equal quality, without gatekeeping at the door. Following that promise across fifty years means following it across borders, because it never stayed in one country. It was carried by a Conservative former Prime Minister to Deng Xiaoping’s China, reworked in India and New York, and folded into the global campaigns that promised education to everyone, everywhere. At each crossing it changed, sometimes beyond recognition, and the people it was originally made for did not always make the journey with it. The same promise is being renewed now, in the language of AI, by a generation with little memory of the earlier attempts.

Few people are better placed to trace the arc than John Daniel. He arrived at the Open University in 1972 as a young lecturer on an internship, ran it as Vice-Chancellor through the 1990s when it counted its students in the hundreds of thousands, and then moved to UNESCO as Assistant Director-General for Education, where he coordinated the global Education for All campaign. When he started out, he says, “I exaggerate only slightly when I say you could have held an international conference in a telephone kiosk for people who were doing distance education seriously. And I just then was swept up in a movement which just grew and grew and grew.”

IThe wager

The Open University’s founding rested on two decisions, and both were fights.

The first concerned scale. The bureaucracy wanted a cautious trial; Walter Perry, the founding Vice-Chancellor, “understood from the start that scale was vital” and insisted on opening with tens of thousands of students. The resistance, Daniel says, “was partly a worry about doing anything big, but also this whole idea was not well received.”

The second concerned esteem. Wilson handed the project to Jennie Lee, his Arts Minister — “a redoubtable Scotswoman” — and, in Daniel’s telling, “she was absolutely determined: this university is going to be as good as any other university, and there’s no compromise on quality.” At a time when roughly six per cent of the age group reached higher education, the wager was that the excluded could be reached with something of full standing, rather than a lesser version prepared for them. These two commitments, scale and parity of esteem, are worth keeping in view throughout what follows, because much of the later history turns on what became of them.

The mockery, meanwhile, had misread the model. The broadcasts amounted to “not more than an hour a week or so of the student’s work”; the other ten hours were reading and writing, built around course materials designed to reflect “modern understanding of how people learn,” with production standards that made “receiving these study units a real pleasure rather than just boring typewritten text.” Perry, Daniel says, “made no bones about the fact that this was by far the most difficult way to get a degree in Britain at the time, because basically you had to do all the work.” The course was the course, and the examiners had never met you.

Daniel draws from this an observation about how the standing of universities is ordinarily measured: “Historically, the measure of quality for many universities has been how tough it is to get into them, not how tough it is to get out of them. One short way of describing the Open University is to say it’s very easy to get in, but much harder to get out with a degree than it would be in a place like Oxford — where once you’re through the door, you’ve got to be really stupid not to manage to come out with a degree.”

IIKeeping the promise

Asked what sustained the promise in practice, Daniel’s answers have little to do with technology and much to do with the culture that grew around it.

“I’ve never been in any organization that carries anything like the same freight of idealism that the Open University did,” he says, and the example he chooses is a warehouse packer rather than a professor: “Even the guys who packed the course materials in the warehouses — those guys really realized that if they put the wrong label on a box, some student was screwed for several weeks before they got it corrected. So there was a real sense that we’re serving these people, from the top of the institution to the bottom.”

▶ Listen — “the guys who packed the course materials”

Television contributed something that later systems would find difficult to reproduce. “In the very early days, if you wanted to study OU Sociology 100, you had to tune in on Thursday evening at seven o’clock. So there was a sense that there were thousands of people around the UK, some students, some not, who were watching this stuff” — taught by faculty who became public figures, Stuart Hall among them. Students dispersed across the country studied simultaneously, and the simultaneity gave a scattered institution something resembling a national cohort. Its loss, as broadcasting fragmented over the following decades, bears on where this story ends.

When the next technology arrived, the institution replayed its own founding argument in miniature. In the early 1990s the university proposed requiring students to use computers, under what was called the Home Computing Policy, and the students’ association resisted on egalitarian grounds: “They made the argument that unless everyone can have it, no one should have it — which is obviously quite an obstacle to any kind of change.” The university responded by giving the student executive computers at home. “What they found was that they could very quickly aggregate student complaints and feed them to us with serious evidence that we had to do something. We were hoist by our own petard.” Two years later the same congress was urging the university to move faster, and Daniel recalls the number of students online rising from about 5,000 in 1995 to something like 150,000 by 1998. The episode compresses into a few years a sequence — resistance on grounds of equity, a small pilot, reversal, then impatience — that organisations adopting AI are now living through at far greater expense.

IIIOpenness in translation

The promise did not stay British, and in moving it changed. What “open” meant, Daniel observes, “was related to the challenges in their own society.”

In Britain, the radical act was the abolition of entry requirements: first come, first served. In the United States, where mature students could already find a place somewhere, Empire State College in New York opened the curriculum instead, allowing students to design their own courses. India, with “far fewer places in universities than there were people wanting to come in,” retained admission criteria while relaxing them. “The definition of openness varied quite widely from one place to another.”

China took the model furthest and altered it most. Edward Heath personally commended the idea to Deng Xiaoping, and the resulting system reached millions, though through assembled classrooms watching closed-circuit television rather than students studying at home. What China set aside was the founding commitment to standing: “China never had the ideal, which was very strong at the Open University, that this university is going to be as good as any university. They were consciously trying to lift the unserved, and they were not in any way pretending to create a new elite university.” The machinery crossed the border; Jennie Lee’s vow did not.

▶ Listen — what China set aside

By Daniel’s count there are now some fifty institutions calling themselves open universities — “some of those probably not much more than a letterhead” — with tens of millions of students between them, more than a million at India’s IGNOU alone, and a Chinese system he puts, across its national and provincial institutions, at perhaps fifty million. What distinguished the successes from the letterheads, in his account, was the founding generation: institutions “created by people who really had fire in their bellies” thrived, while those created because “the central government said, you will create your own university, because we want every state to have one” did not. “That’s not a good way to create anything.” Later, digitalisation gave governments a fresh reason to take an interest in institutions some had half-forgotten: places like Korea realised, as Daniel puts it, “hey, wait a minute, we’ve already got a vehicle we can use to get out into the wider population,” and funded their open universities to carry national systems online. Each society, at each moment, refilled the idea of openness with its own concerns.

It is worth pausing here, because at the same time as open universities were multiplying, the language of openness was being spoken at a much larger scale, and with a very different practical content. Education for All, relaunched at Dakar in 2000 after a first attempt at Jomtien in 1990 “never really went anywhere in the 90s,” promised education to everyone on earth. Discursively, this was the same promise the Open University had made in 1969, extended to the whole world: access for those the existing systems had passed over. Daniel’s own career followed the promise up this widening arc, from running the institution to coordinating the global campaign, arriving at UNESCO in 2001 just after an instructive piece of institutional politics: “The responsibility for implementing the Dakar framework was given to UNESCO, whereas the World Bank was actually expecting it would be given to it. So it started off with a bit of typical international jealousy.”

In practice, however, the global version of the promise had little to do with openness as the OU had understood it, and almost nothing to do with its technology. The campaign concentrated, deliberately, on primary schooling — it was the World Bank, Daniel recalls, who “intelligently said, listen, we can’t take on the whole of K-12 at one shot, so let’s start with primary” — and on conditions more basic than any question of delivery method. Asked whether technology was central to the discussions, he is direct: “No, I don’t think it was central to discussions at all.” What mattered was of another order entirely. “Putting fences around schools was actually quite important, because it meant that the parents of girls were prepared to send them to school.” Teacher absenteeism — “thousands of teachers never showed up for work; they got jobs by influence and patronage, often miles from where they lived” — meant that in many countries “the organization of the system was a total disaster. So it was a case of getting organized, putting some money into materials — very simple, straightforward things.”

The juxtaposition is instructive for anyone tracing how educational promises travel. The same words — open, access, for all — were carrying two different projects at once: in the open university world, a technological route into higher education for adults; in the international campaign, fences, functioning payrolls and primary places for children. Money, rather than rhetoric, marked the difference between the campaign’s two phases. The Millennium Declaration gave the Dakar framework resources, and Daniel spent his UNESCO years trying “to corral the World Bank, UNICEF, all the players, all the national development agencies to contribute.” Cooperation among agencies “is not a given by any means — the Bank considers itself superior to everyone” — but a new World Bank education head, the Kenyan economist Ruth Kagia, “decided she was going to commit to this and really work alongside us,” and for a period “there was quite a hum behind it and good things were happening.” The Scandinavians punched above their weight; Britain funded the annual monitoring report; the Americans “never really joined in any of these things.” By the 2015 target date, in Daniel’s summary, “most countries had got at least ninety per cent of their primary kids into school, with the exceptions being India and Pakistan and Nigeria.” Of the successor framework he is blunt, having been “in at the very beginning of the definition” of the Sustainable Development Goals: “Unlike the Millennium Declaration, which had some money behind it, there’s been very little money behind the Sustainable Development Goals — so a lot of the stuff in higher and other education there is basically whistling in the wind.”

IVMutations

The 2000s produced two further versions of openness, and Daniel had a hand in one and a sceptic’s front-row seat for the other.

The first was open content. In 2002 UNESCO convened the meeting that coined the term “open educational resources,” building on MIT’s decision to place its course notes in the public domain. Progress was “slow, because it was a very scary concept for commercial publishers, and they fought it every inch of the way,” until governments discovered the savings on textbooks, with British Columbia playing an outsized role and the Hewlett Foundation funding the movement into maturity. Alongside it ran the campaign to open research itself, of which Daniel offers a tidy description: “The scientific journals are a real racket, where people make a huge amount of money by charging you to publish your articles and then charging other people to read them.”

The second was the MOOC, and here the question of who the promise reaches — the question this series returns to in every episode — produces its clearest answer. Daniel writes as a participant rather than an onlooker; he has taken twenty MOOCs himself (“I didn’t finish all of them because I got bored, but many I did finish”). His assessment is generous about the form and pointed about the beneficiary: “The people who do them are self-chosen, and probably pretty bright and pretty qualified. There was a study done that most people studying MOOCs already had a degree. So it was a kind of more to those with most.”

▶ Listen — “more to those with most”

Set against Jennie Lee’s founding vow, the distance travelled is considerable. The Open University was engineered to reach “people without the usual school qualifications,” and, Daniel says, “that has tended to be lost in most attempts to imitate it.” He describes the general mechanism in terms that reach well beyond MOOCs: “As people have picked this up, they’ve usually tended to offer more bells and whistles on their existing offer to their existing students, rather than to try and reach a new clientele.” The pandemic demonstrated the same tendency at global scale. Universities “did a remarkable job of adapting,” but “when you had to change overnight to offering your courses on Zoom, you basically offered it to the students you already had. You weren’t trying to spread your wings into the wider community. And I think that’s still the case to this day.”

What MOOCs changed permanently, Daniel notes, was the unit of learning: they “made it credible to offer much shorter snippets of courses,” opening the way to micro-credentials and to “a whole industry where people who do not have official credentialing power get in and, by their own credibility, back micro-credentials. It’s very much a buyer-beware kind of business, but nevertheless a useful addition to the ecosystem.” A promise that had originally concerned who could enter education had evolved, through successive translations, into a market in ever-smaller units of it.

VThe record

Fifty years on, the ledger can be drawn up. The system was permanently loosened: “You didn’t have to apologize for doing something which was new and a bit odd.” Flexible, part-time, distance and online study moved from a national joke to ordinary infrastructure across much of the world, and the case for scale no longer needs making.

Other parts of the promise fared less well. Parity of esteem eroded as elite institutions, “as always, doing their best to make a comeback,” reasserted an older equation of quality with “restricting entry.” The cohort dissolved along with broadcast simultaneity: “If you lose scale, you tend to lose the notion of cohorts and people studying at the same time. So it does become a more individualistic kind of thing.” And the original clientele fell away gradually, as each imitation kept the methods and redirected them towards students it already had.

Daniel returns more than once to a founding irony. “When the OU began in the 1970s, higher education in the UK was free — for everyone who went to a campus university. But it was never free at the Open University, which is kind of a bit backwards.” The one institution built for those with least was the one that charged, and Perry “had to fight this all the time.” Nor does Daniel believe the animating idea retains its former force. “There’s a terrific emphasis these days in the press on inequality,” he says, but openness as a live political pressure has faded: “It’s a rhetorical concern to politicians, but you don’t get the impression that there are the same pressures.”

He then offers the observation that, more than any other in the conversation, explains why revisiting these episodes matters. Institutions forget. “The contemporary UNESCO doesn’t have an institutional memory,” he says. He belongs to a UNESCO retirees group that meets in Paris every other month: “These guys are reliving what was hot in the 60s, 70s, 80s, 90s. It’s very, very interesting — but it doesn’t carry over much into the present day.” He contrasts it with the BBC, which he found had “no institutional memory whatsoever” when the OU worked with it, and with the OU itself, where “nothing ever got forgotten.” The experience of half a century of educational promise-making now sits with a group of pensioners, meeting a short walk from the offices where the next set of promises is being drafted.

▶ Listen — where UNESCO’s memory now lives

The consequences are visible in the succession of global commitments. Universal education was promised for Africa at Addis Ababa in 1961, for the world at Jomtien in 1990, again at Dakar in 2000 and at Incheon in 2015, and it is under negotiation once more in the post-2030 discussions — in many cases by people with limited knowledge of what became of the earlier attempts, and without recourse to those who carried them out.

VIThe newest promise

Ask Daniel about AI and he begins with a disclaimer — “I’ve decided I’m not really going to get myself into AI in any big way… you need to find someone else, Alex, if you want some really decent insights” — before offering, in the space of a minute, two remarks that bear directly on everything above.

The first concerns direction. “We’re in an era where the emphasis is more on individual striving than on collective advancement.” The OU aimed at individuals studying at home, yet produced “a great sense of collective achievement”; AI-personalised learning proposes a curriculum of one, which sits at the opposite end of that spectrum from Sociology 100 at seven o’clock on a Thursday evening.

The second is a warning, delivered with the flatness of long experience: “I think it’s all to try for. I think we’re headed for some fairly major busts on the way. And I think there’s a very great danger that it will increase inequalities rather than the reverse. But who knows?”

▶ Listen — Sir John’s warning on AI

The record he has laid out gives that warning its content. A technology arrives promising to open learning to everyone; its early adopters prove to be the already educated; institutions deploy it for the students and staff they already have; early resistance gives way to impatient demand; the rhetoric of access runs ahead of the money. Whether the present promise escapes this sequence will depend on whether anyone asks, early and with evidence, who is actually being reached — the question the Open University’s founders answered by design, and the question that each of the promise’s later translations quietly set aside.

None of this makes the new promise false. The Open University stands as evidence that the extravagant version can be kept, and also as a record of what keeping it required: derision absorbed, two commitments defended against constant pressure, a culture in which the warehouse packer understood the mission, and several decades of institutional patience. Each era attaches its highest hopes to learning, and each country reworks those hopes in its own terms; the promises now being made for AI are being made in many countries at once, in similar language, at unusual speed. The history recounted here suggests that their outcome will turn less on the technology than on whether anyone holds them, as Jennie Lee held hers, to the standard of being as good as any other, for the people who have least.

Sir John Daniel

Sir John Daniel was Vice-Chancellor of the Open University (1990–2001), Assistant Director-General for Education at UNESCO (2001–2004), and President of the Commonwealth of Learning (2004–2012) — a career spent, across three continents, on the promise of open and distance learning. This conversation is the first in The Reform Cycle, a series tracing the promises we attach to learning: how they are made, kept and broken across time, through conversations with the people who made them.
